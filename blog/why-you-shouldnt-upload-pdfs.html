Why You Shouldn't Upload PDFs to Random Websites

Published by the {{#if brandName}}{{brandName}}{{else}}BentoPDF{{/if}} team

Every day, millions of people Google "merge PDF" or "compress PDF" and click the first result. They upload their tax returns, employment contracts, medical records, and legal documents to a website they've never heard of before. Most don't think twice about it.

Here's what actually happens when you upload a PDF to a typical online tool:

What happens to your file

When you drag a PDF onto iLovePDF, Smallpdf, or most online PDF tools, the file is transmitted over the internet to their servers. The server processes your file — merges it, compresses it, converts it — and then sends the result back to you. Your original file sits on their server until they delete it.

Most services claim to delete files within 1-2 hours. But here's the thing: you have no way to verify that. You're trusting a company you found on Google five minutes ago with potentially sensitive documents.

The real risks

Data breaches: If the service gets hacked, your documents could be exposed. This isn't hypothetical — data breaches happen to companies of every size, every year.

Employee access: Even if the company has good intentions, employees with server access could theoretically view your files.

Third-party processing: Some services use cloud providers like AWS or Google Cloud to process files. Your document might pass through multiple systems before coming back to you.

Metadata retention: Even if the file is deleted, metadata (filename, file size, timestamps, your IP address) is often logged and retained.

The alternative: browser-based processing

Modern web technology has made it possible to process PDFs entirely in your browser. Using WebAssembly (Wasm), the same libraries that power desktop PDF software can now run at near-native speed inside a browser tab.

When you use a browser-based PDF tool, your file never leaves your device. The processing happens on your CPU, in your browser's memory. When you close the tab, everything is gone. There's no server, no upload, no risk of a data breach.

How to verify a tool doesn't upload your files

Here's a simple test you can do with any online PDF tool:

  1. Open the tool in your browser
  2. Press F12 to open Developer Tools
  3. Go to the Network tab
  4. Clear the network log
  5. Upload and process a PDF file
  6. Look at the network requests

If you see large outgoing requests (matching your file size), the tool is uploading your file. If you only see small requests for static assets, the tool is processing locally.

Try this with our merge tool — you'll see zero outgoing data requests. Then try it with any server-based tool and compare the difference.

Bottom line

If you're processing non-sensitive documents — a meme, a public flyer, a recipe — it doesn't matter where you process them. But if you're handling contracts, financial documents, medical records, legal papers, or anything you wouldn't want a stranger to read, use a tool that processes files locally.
